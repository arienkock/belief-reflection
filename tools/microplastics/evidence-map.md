# Microplastics — Origin and Effects — evidence map (v1)
_Belief-investigation tool. Generated by scripts/compose-tool.py. Nodes are **questions**; only leaf nodes carry a claim you rate yourself. The tool shows evidence and its limits — it does **not** compute a verdict or update your number for you._

> **What this map leaves out (scope):** Authored dimensions: measurement/detection reliability, environmental origin, human-health association, controlled human-harm evidence, ecological harm, and source policy. DELIBERATELY NOT COVERED (invisible to this tool): the plastic-additive chemicals that leach from particles (bisphenols, phthalates, flame retardants) as a separate toxicology, scored only where a health finding bundles them; nanoplastics as a distinct class except where a cited study reports micro- AND nano-plastics together; airborne/indoor exposure and ingestion-vs-inhalation route apportionment beyond what a finding carries; the climate/carbon footprint of plastics; macroplastic entanglement and ingestion (turtles, seabirds) as distinct from micro-scale effects; economic-cost estimates; and whether plastic production or use SHOULD be restricted all things considered — a values question this tool records no verdict on. Absence is a scope choice, not evidence of no effect. Three definitional boundaries: 'present' is not 'harmful' (detecting a particle in a tissue is not evidence it damages it); 'harm at high laboratory dose' is not 'harm at environmental concentration'; and 'microplastic' (<5 mm) is not 'nanoplastic' (<1 µm) — findings must not silently swap them.

### ▸ What does the evidence show about microplastics — where they come from, whether we can measure them, and what they do to health and the environment?
<sub>`n_root` · topic · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
> ⚠ presence is far better evidenced than harm — confidence on 'microplastics are everywhere' does not transfer to 'microplastics are harming us'
> **Depends on:** 'Harming us' is not one measurable quantity. The literature separately measures whether microplastic concentrations can be measured consistently at all, whether the particles are reliably found inside the human body or are largely laboratory contamination, where they originate, whether human burden is associated with disease, whether controlled studies show harm at realistic doses, whether wild organisms are harmed at realistic concentrations, and what source policy changes — with sharply different degrees of consensus, and with the PRESENCE questions far better evidenced than the HARM questions. Public debate routinely lets 'microplastics are everywhere' stand in for 'microplastics are hurting you'; this tree exists to keep them apart.
- **◑ COMPLICATES** · review · strong — There is no single measurement of 'are microplastics harming us'. The literature separately measures whether concentrations can be measured reproducibly, whether particles are genuinely inside the body, where they originate, whether human burden is associated with disease, whether controlled models show harm at realistic doses, and whether wild organisms are harmed at realistic concentrations. Authoritative weight-of-evidence reviews conclude that, at current environmental concentrations, widespread population-level human harm has not been demonstrated — while stressing large uncertainties and the need for long-term monitoring. Presence is far better established than harm.

- **◑ COMPLICATES** · experimental · moderate — Confidence is unevenly earned across the sub-questions, and the weakest link is measurement. Every downstream exposure and association number inherits that error; confidence earned on a well-studied sub-question does not transfer to the others.
  <sub>src: Ciornii et al. 2025, Interlaboratory Comparison Reveals State of the Art in Microplastic Detection and Quantification Methods, Analytical Chemistry 97:8719.   ### ▸ Can microplastic concentrations, and their presence inside the human body, be measured reliably — or are the numbers method-dependent and vulnerable to contamination?
  <sub>`n_measure` · dimension · 🟡 thin</sub>
  > ⚠ measurement validity is itself contested — read every exposure and harm number in that light
  > **Depends on:** Two separable measurement questions. First, whether different laboratories analysing the same sample get consistent concentrations (cross-lab reproducibility) — if not, every downstream exposure number carries that error. Second, whether particles reported inside human tissue are genuinely there rather than contamination introduced during sampling and analysis, given that plastics are ubiquitous in labs. A method can be irreproducible across labs yet still detect something real, or a detection can be real yet the concentration wrong — the two failures are different and are scored separately.
  > *Scope note:* Covers cross-laboratory reproducibility of quantification and the contamination-vs-real-signal question for human-tissue detection. Does not adjudicate any single analytical technique (Pyr-GC/MS vs FTIR vs Raman vs Nile Red) as a whole, nor the separate question of what size classes current methods can even see.
  - **◑ COMPLICATES** · review · moderate — This dimension has two distinct failure modes that must not be conflated. Measurement can be UNRELIABLE (different laboratories analysing the same sample report very different concentrations) or CONTAMINATED (a detection inside a tissue is real-looking but introduced by ubiquitous laboratory plastic). They are separate questions with separate evidence: a method can be irreproducible across labs yet still detect something real, and a detection can be genuine yet its concentration wrong.
    <sub>src: Systematic review of microplastics and nanoplastics in human tissues: evidence, analytical protocols, and methodological challenges, 2026 (ScienceDirect S2666831926000081). [contamination-review] · caveat: A framing of the two failure modes drawn from the methodological literature, not a single measurement.</sub>

    ### • Are microplastics reliably detected inside the human body (blood, placenta, tissue), rather than being sampling or laboratory contamination?
    <sub>`n_measure_body` · leaf · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Microplastics are reliably detected inside the human body (blood, placenta, tissues) at levels indicating genuine internal exposure — the detections reflect particles that were in the body, not contamination introduced during sampling and analysis.**
    > ⚠ all human-tissue detections inherit a contamination risk; concentrations from studies without rigorous procedural blanks may be overestimates
    > **Depends on:** Depends on the analytical quality control: because plastics pervade laboratory air, clothing, and labware, a detection is only credible if procedural blanks, blank-correction, and anti-contamination controls rule out external plastic — and if the polymers are confirmed spectroscopically rather than by stain alone (tissue lipids and proteins are known false positives). Early studies often lacked these controls; studies that added them tend to report lower, more realistic concentrations. So the leaf turns on whether the positive detections survive contamination control, not on whether particles were reported.
    - **▲ FOR** · observational · moderate — Using a validated double-shot pyrolysis–GC/MS method for particles ≥700 nm, plastic was quantified in the whole blood of 17 of 22 healthy donors (~77%), at a mean summed concentration of 1.6 µg/mL; PET, polyethylene, polymers of styrene, and PMMA were the most common. This was the first mass-based quantification of the polymeric component of plastic in human blood, indicating that particles reach the systemic circulation.
      <sub>effect: quantifiable plastic in 17/22 donors; mean 1.6 µg/mL · src: Leslie et al. 2022, Discovery and quantification of plastic particle pollution in human blood, Environment International 163:107199. [blood-leslie] · caveat: Small n (22); a first-of-kind method; like all such studies, background plastic contamination is a standing concern the authors worked to control but cannot fully exclude. · ⚡ F-cause-1, F-rel-1</sub>
    - **▲ FOR** · observational · thin — Raman microspectroscopy of six human placentas from physiological pregnancies detected 12 microplastic fragments (5–10 µm) in four of the six — on the fetal side, the maternal side, and the chorioamniotic membranes. All were pigmented; three were identified as stained polypropylene. This was the first report of microplastics in the human placenta, a second independent tissue and team supporting genuine internal presence.
      <sub>effect: 12 fragments (5–10 µm) in 4 of 6 placentas · src: Ragusa et al. 2021, Plasticenta: First evidence of microplastics in human placenta, Environment International 146:106274. [placenta-ragusa] · caveat: Very small pilot (6 placentas); only a fraction of each was analysed; contamination controls were limited — a discovery study, not a prevalence estimate.</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · review · moderate — Because plastics pervade laboratory air, clothing, and labware, human-tissue detections are vulnerable to false positives: methodological reviews find that failing to run and report procedural blanks leads to overestimation, that tissue lipids and proteins are known false positives for plastic, and that stain-based methods (e.g. Nile Red) mark non-plastic organic matter. Notably, studies that added rigorous multi-blank controls — mostly after 2023 — generally reported LOWER, more realistic concentrations, implying part of the earlier signal was contamination.
      <sub>effect: qualitative; rigorous-blank studies report lower concentrations · src: Systematic review of microplastics and nanoplastics in human tissues: evidence, analytical protocols, and methodological challenges, 2026 (ScienceDirect S2666831926000081); analytical-challenges reviews. [contamination-review] · caveat: A methodological critique, not a claim that no microplastics are present in the body; it argues that concentrations are overstated and some detections artefactual, not that the phenomenon is zero. · ⚡ F-assoc-1</sub>
  ### ▸ Where do microplastics come from, and are the sources quantified well enough to act on?
  <sub>`n_origin` · dimension · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
  > ⚠ source apportionment covers primary microplastics (15–31% of the total); the dominant secondary fraction is poorly quantified
  > **Depends on:** That microplastics are human-made is not in dispute; the live questions are which sources dominate, how confidently they are quantified, and whether the environmentally dominant fraction (secondary particles from the fragmentation of larger plastic waste) can be attributed at all. Source-apportionment estimates are model-dependent and carry wide uncertainty, so 'we know where they come from' can be true at the level of naming sources yet weak at the level of quantifying and ranking them for policy.
  > *Scope note:* Covers whether dominant sources are identified and quantified well enough to prioritise. Does not separately score airborne vs waterborne pathways, marine vs freshwater vs terrestrial partitioning, or the fate/transport of particles once released — only where a finding carries them.
  - **◑ COMPLICATES** · theoretical · moderate — That microplastics are human-made is not in dispute; the live questions are which sources dominate, how confidently they are quantified, and whether the environmentally dominant fraction can be attributed at all. Source-apportionment inventories cover PRIMARY microplastics (deliberately made, or worn from products) but the most abundant environmental microplastic is SECONDARY — fragments of larger mismanaged plastic waste — which is poorly quantified. So 'we know where they come from' is stronger for naming and ranking primary sources than for quantifying the total.
    <sub>src: Boucher & Friot 2017, IUCN (primary vs secondary framing); microplastic source-apportionment literature. [secondary-dominance] · caveat: A framing of the primary/secondary distinction and its measurement asymmetry, not a standalone measurement.</sub>

    > ⚠ FOR evidence is a modelled global apportionment with wide uncertainty; regional source shares differ, and it addresses only the primary fraction
    > **Depends on:** Depends on whether source-apportionment inventories converge on a small set of dominant, actionable sources — and on how the dominant ENVIRONMENTAL fraction (secondary fragmentation) is handled.     - **▼ AGAINST** · theoretical · moderate — The claim strains at the level of the TOTAL.   ### ▸ Do microplastics harm human health — is there a measured association with disease, and does controlled evidence show harm at realistic exposure?
  <sub>`n_health` · dimension · 🟡 thin</sub>
  > ⚠ the human-harm evidence is early and thin; neither presence in the body nor high-dose laboratory harm establishes harm at real exposure
  > **Depends on:** Two separable questions with different designs. Whether human microplastic burden is ASSOCIATED with disease rests on observational human studies — currently essentially one contested study. Whether microplastics CAUSE harm rests on controlled animal and in-vitro models, where the pivotal issue is dose: most experiments use concentrations far above real human exposure. Neither the presence of particles in the body nor harm at a high laboratory dose establishes harm at the amounts people actually encounter.
  > *Scope note:* Covers the observational human association and controlled-model causal evidence for microplastic PARTICLES. The toxicology of additive chemicals that leach from plastics (bisphenols, phthalates) is out of scope except where a finding bundles them. Long-term human disease endpoints cannot be tested experimentally in people, so the causal leaf relies on animal/in-vitro models with the attendant extrapolation caveat.
  - **◑ COMPLICATES** · theoretical · moderate — Two separable questions govern this dimension, and both are weak. Whether human burden is ASSOCIATED with disease rests on observational human studies — currently essentially one contested study. Whether microplastics CAUSE harm rests on animal and in-vitro models where the pivotal problem is dose. Crucially, neither finding a particle in a tissue nor demonstrating harm at a high laboratory dose establishes harm at the amounts people actually encounter — the presence-to-harm and high-dose-to-real-dose steps are where the evidence is thinnest.
    <sub>src: SAPEA 2019 (weight-of-evidence on human health); WHO 2019, Microplastics in drinking-water. [sapea] · caveat: A framing of the two health questions and the two inferential gaps, not a measurement.</sub>
    ### • Is higher microplastic exposure or internal burden associated with worse human health outcomes?
    <sub>`n_health_assoc` · leaf · 🟡 thin</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Higher microplastic exposure or internal burden is associated with worse human health outcomes (e.g. cardiovascular events).**
    > ⚠ rests essentially on one contested observational study (Marfella 2024); no independent replication located | fast-moving: Re-search for independent replications of Marfella 2024 before any republish — this leaf rests on a single 2024 study and would move if it is replicated or refuted.
    > **Depends on:** Depends almost entirely on one study: Marfella et al. (NEJM 2024) found carotid-plaque patients with detectable micro- and nanoplastics had a ~4.5× rate of heart attack, stroke, or death over ~34 months. It is a single observational cohort whose exposure measurement drew formal contamination critiques, and the authors state it does not prove causation. There is no body of replication; the leaf is genuinely thin, and whether the association is real, confounded, or a measurement artefact is unresolved.
    - **▲ FOR** · observational · moderate — In a prospective, multicentre observational study of 257 patients undergoing carotid endarterectomy, micro- and nanoplastics were detected in plaque of most patients (polyethylene in 58.4%, mean 21.7±24.5 µg/mg; PVC in 12.1%). Over a mean 34 months, patients with detectable plastics had a hazard ratio of 4.53 (95% CI 2.00–10.27) for the composite of myocardial infarction, stroke, or death from any cause — the first human study linking measured microplastic burden to hard cardiovascular outcomes.
      <sub>effect: HR 4.53 (95% CI 2.00–10.27) for MI/stroke/death; MNPs in 58.4% · src: Marfella et al. 2024, Microplastics and Nanoplastics in Atheromas and Cardiovascular Events, New England Journal of Medicine 390:900–910.     - **▼ AGAINST** · theoretical · moderate — Formal correspondence in the same journal challenged whether the plaque microplastic measurement is credible: no pre-analytical anti-contamination procedures beyond glass tubes were described, the operating-room environment and surgical equipment themselves contain polyethylene and PVC (creating high intraoperative contamination risk), and no operating-room blank samples were tested.       <sub>effect: qualitative; contamination-control critique · src: Correspondence on Marfella et al., New England Journal of Medicine, 9 May 2024 (letters incl. NEJMc2404154), and authors' reply. [marfella-critique] · caveat: A methodological critique, not a competing study; the authors replied defending their controls. It raises reasonable doubt about the exposure measurement, it does not disprove the association.</sub>
    - **◑ COMPLICATES** · review · moderate — The broader human evidence base is essentially this one study: there is no replication in an independent population, and authoritative weight-of-evidence reviews (SAPEA 2019; WHO 2019) conclude that a population-level human health effect of microplastics has not been demonstrated at current exposures. A single striking observational association, however large the hazard ratio, cannot on its own carry a general claim that microplastic burden is associated with worse health.

    ### • Does controlled (animal / in-vitro) evidence show microplastics cause biological harm at doses relevant to real human exposure?
    <sub>`n_health_cause` · leaf · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Controlled animal and in-vitro evidence shows microplastics cause biological harm (inflammation, oxidative stress, organ or reproductive effects) at doses relevant to real human exposure.**
    > ⚠ controlled harm is largely demonstrated at doses far above real human exposure — 'harm at a realistic dose' is the contested part | many studies use uniform polystyrene microspheres, unlike environmental particles
    > **Depends on:** Two parts that must not be conflated: whether controlled models show harm at ALL (they frequently do — inflammation, oxidative stress, gut and reproductive effects), and whether they show it at REALISTIC doses (mostly they do not). A meta-analysis found only ~17% of tested exposure concentrations occur in the real environment; nanoplastic doses in studies run orders of magnitude above estimated human intake. Weight-of-evidence reviews (SAPEA 2019; WHO 2019) conclude harm at current human exposure has not been demonstrated. So a real effect at a high dose does not license a claim about everyday exposure.
    - **▲ FOR** · review · moderate — Controlled animal and in-vitro studies repeatedly demonstrate that microplastics and nanoplastics CAN cause biological harm: reviews of toxicological pathways report inflammation, oxidative stress, and gut, metabolic, and reproductive effects across rodent and cellular models. Mechanistically, small enough particles translocate across biological barriers and provoke immune and oxidative responses — establishing biological plausibility that microplastics are not inert.
      <sub>effect: qualitative; harm demonstrated across models (mostly high-dose) · src: Exploring toxicological pathways of microplastics and nanoplastics: insights from animal and cellular models, Journal of Hazardous Materials 2025 (S0304389425007095). Two authoritative reviews, reading the controlled and exposure literature, decline to affirm harm at real exposure.
      <sub>effect: qualitative; no widespread risk demonstrated at current exposure · src: SAPEA 2019, A Scientific Perspective on Microplastics in Nature and Society; WHO 2019, Microplastics in drinking-water.   ### ▸ Do microplastics measurably harm wild organisms at the concentrations actually found in the environment?
  <sub>`n_environ` · dimension · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
  > ⚠ the ecological evidence base is larger than the human one but shares the concentration-realism problem
  > **Depends on:** The ecological evidence base is larger and older than the human-health one, but it shares the dose problem: many demonstrated effects occur only at concentrations well above those measured in most of the environment. So the question is not merely 'can microplastics harm organisms' (they can, in the lab) but whether harm occurs at environmentally realistic concentrations, and whether risk is widespread or concentrated in pollution hotspots.
  > *Scope note:* Covers harm to aquatic (marine and freshwater) organisms at realistic concentrations, the best-studied ecological compartment. Terrestrial/soil ecosystems, whole-ecosystem and food-web effects, and macroplastic entanglement/ingestion are out of scope except where a finding carries them.
  - **◑ COMPLICATES** · theoretical · moderate — The ecological evidence base is larger and older than the human-health one, but it inherits the same dose problem. Laboratory studies establish that microplastics CAN harm organisms, yet many demonstrated effects occur only at concentrations above those measured across most of the environment. So the ecological question is not 'can microplastics harm wildlife' (they can) but whether harm occurs at environmentally realistic concentrations, and whether risk is widespread or concentrated in pollution hotspots.
    <sub>src: Bucci et al. 2020, Ecological Applications 30(2):e02044 (concentration-realism framing). [bucci-rochman] · caveat: A framing of the concentration-realism problem for ecology, not a standalone effect estimate.</sub>
    ### • Do microplastics cause measurable harm to aquatic organisms at environmentally realistic concentrations?
    <sub>`n_environ_wildlife` · leaf · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Microplastics cause measurable harm to aquatic organisms (reduced feeding, growth, reproduction, or survival) at concentrations actually found in the environment — not only at inflated laboratory doses.**
    > ⚠ demonstrated effects are common but mostly at concentrations above ambient; harm at environmentally realistic concentrations is the contested part
    > **Depends on:** Depends on reconciling meta-analytic evidence of real organismal effects (reduced consumption, growth, reproduction, survival across taxa) with the finding that most of those effects were measured at unrealistically high concentrations, and with risk-assessment work (species-sensitivity distributions) suggesting modelled thresholds are exceeded only in a minority of sampled locations. Effects are real and variable across taxa; whether they bite at today's ambient concentrations, and where, is the contested part.
    - **▲ FOR** · review · strong — A meta-analysis of microplastic effects on fish and aquatic invertebrates found negative effects on consumption, growth, reproduction, and survival for at least some taxa, with reduced consumption of natural prey the most consistent effect; zooplankton were flagged as particularly susceptible, with potential food-web ramifications. Real organismal harm from microplastic exposure is demonstrable, not merely hypothesised.
      <sub>effect: negative effects on consumption/growth/reproduction/survival (taxon-dependent); consumption most consistent · src: Foley et al. 2018, A meta-analysis of the effects of exposure to microplastics on fish and aquatic invertebrates, Science of the Total Environment 631–632:550–559. [foley-meta] · caveat: The authors stress effects were 'highly variable across taxa' and many were neutral; the meta-analysis pools studies many of which used high exposure concentrations, so it establishes harm exists more than harm at ambient levels. · ⚡ F-wild-2, F-wild-3</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · review · strong — A meta-analysis and systematic review of 139 studies testing 577 independent effects found that only ~17% of the exposure concentrations used were environmentally relevant — i.e. the great majority of demonstrated ecological effects occur at plastic concentrations higher than those found across most of the environment. The evidence that microplastics harm organisms AT REALISTIC CONCENTRATIONS is therefore much weaker than the raw count of 'effect found' studies implies.
      <sub>effect: only ~17% of tested concentrations environmentally relevant (139 studies, 577 effects) · src: Bucci, Tulio & Rochman 2020, What is known and unknown about the effects of plastic pollution: A meta-analysis and systematic review, Ecological Applications 30(2):e02044. [bucci-rochman] · caveat: 'Mostly tested at unrealistic concentrations' is a critique of the evidence base, not proof of no effect at ambient levels; the same review documents that effects are common when they are tested. · ⚡ F-cause-2, F-wild-1</sub>

  ### ▸ What would microplastic policy — source bans such as microbead bans — measurably change?
  <sub>`n_policy` · dimension · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>

  > **Depends on:** Depends on matching the instrument to the target.
