# Proposal: Microplastics — Origin and Effects

_Proposed 2026-07-15. Status: **draft — pre-Phase-1**. The dimension sketch
below is the pre-freeze draft; once nodes are frozen it will be superseded by
`nodes.json` and `phase1-to-phase2-handoff.md`, and where they differ the
frozen tree and the handoff's decision log govern._

## Working title and scope

**Microplastics: origin and effects.** One sentence: what does the evidence
actually show about where microplastics come from, whether we can measure
them and our exposure to them reliably, and what they do to human health and
to ecosystems — separating the well-established fact that the particles are
*present and human-made* from the much weaker evidence that they are
*harmful at real-world exposure levels*.

Chosen this session at the user's request (fourth tool, after Immigration,
Social Media & Teen Mental Health, and Ultra-Processed Foods).

## Why this topic fits the tool

The public conversation treats "microplastics are everywhere," "microplastics
are in our blood and placentas," "microplastics cause disease," and
"microplastics are wrecking ecosystems" as a single alarming claim. They are
in fact separately-measured questions with sharply different evidence
quality, and the strongest rhetorical move in the debate — presence implies
harm — is exactly the conflation this tool format exists to pull apart.

1. **Splits into an honest question tree.** "Are microplastics bad?" hides at
   least six separately-measured questions: (a) can microplastic
   concentrations even be *measured* consistently across labs; (b) are the
   particles *reliably detected inside the human body*, or is much of that
   signal laboratory contamination; (c) are the environmental *sources*
   identified and quantified well enough to act on; (d) is human microplastic
   burden *associated* with disease; (e) does controlled (animal / in-vitro)
   evidence show *harm at environmentally realistic doses*; (f) do
   microplastics *harm wild organisms* at realistic concentrations; and what
   *source policy* measurably changes. These have different answers and
   different evidence quality.

2. **Genuinely two-sided at high tiers, in an unusual shape.** Unlike most
   topics, here the *presence/origin* side is strong and the *effects* side
   is weak — and the honest tool has to resist letting the well-evidenced
   presence findings vouch for the poorly-evidenced harm findings. There is a
   headline human cohort-adjacent study (Marfella et al., *NEJM* 2024:
   microplastics in carotid plaque associated with a 4.5× rate of cardiac
   events) set against formal published critiques of that same study's
   contamination controls; toxicological reviews reporting harm set against a
   meta-analytic finding that ~83% of exposure experiments used
   concentrations never seen in nature; and authoritative weight-of-evidence
   reviews (SAPEA 2019; WHO 2019) concluding that population-level human harm
   has *not* been demonstrated at current exposures while stressing large
   uncertainties.

3. **A measurement/definition problem promoted to its own scored leaf.**
   Exactly as the Ultra-Processed Foods tool made NOVA inter-rater
   reliability a scored leaf rather than an unscored fog, this tool scores
   whether microplastic quantification is even reproducible across labs (a
   VAMAS interlaboratory comparison of 84 labs found reproducibility spanning
   roughly 46–129%). If the exposure metric is unreliable, every downstream
   association inherits that error — so the participant rates it directly
   instead of having it hang unscored over everything.

4. **The evidence base is moving fast and policy is being written.** The EU
   restricted intentionally-added microplastics (2023); measurement
   standardisation efforts (ISO/VAMAS) are active; and the human-health
   literature is only a few years old (blood 2022, placenta 2021, plaque
   2024). A snapshot now captures a live, contested moment.

## Fit against the candidate-topic criteria

- **Splits into an honest question tree** — yes; see the six sub-questions
  above and the [source scan](source-scan.md), which sources each draft leaf
  in both directions (or flags it thin in advance).
- **A scored leaf can go either way on the literature** — most leaves are
  genuinely two-sided. Two are expected to be honestly one-sided and are
  named now, not padded later: the measurement-reliability leaf (the
  well-evidenced side is the *unreliable* verdict; the "reproducible" side is
  thin), and the source-policy leaf (a directly-measured "labels/bans don't
  work" counter-study was not located).
- **Not primarily moral or definitional** — the *measurement* dispute is real
  and is scored as one leaf and bounded by scope notes rather than pretended
  away. The core of the topic is measured quantities: source apportionment,
  detection concentrations, hazard ratios, ecological effect sizes. Whether
  plastic production *should* be curtailed all things considered is a values
  question handled as a scope boundary, not scored.

## Draft root question

> **What does the evidence show about microplastics — where they come from,
> whether we can measure them, and what they do to health and the
> environment?**

(Simple register: *Are microplastics harming us?*)

Root `depends_on`: "harming us" is not one measurable quantity. The
literature separately measures whether microplastic concentrations can be
measured consistently at all, whether the particles are reliably found inside
the human body, where they originate, whether human burden is associated with
disease, whether controlled studies show harm at realistic doses, whether
wild organisms are harmed at realistic concentrations, and what source policy
changes — with sharply different degrees of consensus, and with the
*presence* questions far better evidenced than the *harm* questions.

## Expected dimensions (draft, not frozen)

1. **`d_measure` — Can we even measure microplastics and our exposure?**
   - Microplastic concentrations can be measured consistently across
     laboratories and methods (reproducibility).
   - Microplastics are reliably detected *inside the human body* (blood,
     placenta, tissue) at levels indicating genuine internal exposure — not
     sampling/laboratory contamination.
2. **`d_origin` — Where do microplastics come from?**
   - The major sources of microplastic pollution are identified and
     quantified well enough to target the biggest contributors (tyre wear,
     synthetic textiles, fragmentation of larger plastic).
3. **`d_health` — Human health effects.**
   - Higher microplastic exposure / internal burden is associated with worse
     human health outcomes.
   - Controlled (animal / in-vitro) evidence shows microplastics cause
     biological harm at doses relevant to real human exposure.
4. **`d_environ` — Environmental / ecological effects.**
   - Microplastics cause measurable harm to aquatic organisms at
     environmentally realistic concentrations.
5. **`d_policy` — What would microplastic policy change?**
   - Policies targeting microplastic sources (e.g. microbead bans)
     measurably reduce microplastic pollution.

Roughly 7 scored leaves across 5 dimensions — a touch leaner than the prior
tools (8–11 leaves / 4 dimensions), which suits a topic whose harm evidence
is genuinely sparse: better to score fewer questions honestly than
manufacture leaves to fill a grid.

## Coverage note (what this map deliberately leaves out)

DELIBERATELY NOT COVERED (invisible to this tool): the **plastic-additive
chemicals** that leach from particles (bisphenols, phthalates, flame
retardants) as a separate toxicology — a large literature of its own, scored
here only where a health finding bundles it; **nanoplastics** as a distinct
class except where a cited study reports micro- *and* nano-plastics together
(findings must state which); **airborne indoor exposure** and route
apportionment (ingestion vs inhalation) beyond what a finding carries;
**climate / carbon footprint** of plastics; **macroplastic** entanglement and
ingestion (turtles, seabirds) as distinct from micro-scale effects;
**economic cost** estimates; and whether plastic production or use *should* be
restricted all things considered — a values question the tool records no
verdict on. Absence is a scope choice, not evidence of no effect.

Three definitional boundaries to hold: (a) **"present" is not "harmful"** —
detecting particles in a tissue is not evidence they damage it; (b) **"harm at
high laboratory dose" is not "harm at environmental concentration"** —
findings must state the exposure level; (c) **"microplastic" (<5 mm) is not
"nanoplastic" (<1 µm)** — the size class governs whether particles cross
biological barriers, so it must be stated.

## Known sourcing hazards (flag at authoring time)

- **Presence-implies-harm drift.** The single biggest hazard. Detection
  findings (blood, placenta, plaque) are evidentially about *presence*; they
  must not be written, in either register, as if they establish *harm*. The
  simple register is where this drift will try to enter (I7).
- **The contamination problem is load-bearing on two leaves.** Plastics are
  ubiquitous in labs; procedural-blank failures produce false positives. The
  detection leaf and the human-association leaf both depend on whether the
  measurement survived contamination control — carry the critique, do not
  bury it.
- **Dose/concentration realism.** Most toxicology and ecology studies use
  concentrations far above environmental levels; a meta-analysis found only
  ~17% of tested concentrations are environmentally realistic. Every
  "causes harm" finding must state whether the dose was realistic.
- **Single-study leaves.** The human-health-association leaf rests
  essentially on one contested study (Marfella 2024). Flag thin; do not
  imply a body of replication that does not exist.
- **Nutrient-label-style transportability trap (policy).** As with the Chile
  label leaf in the UPF tool, the strong policy evidence targets a *narrow*
  source (personal-care microbeads ≈ 2% of primary microplastics), while the
  environmental total is dominated by secondary fragmentation — so "a
  targeted ban reduced its target" does not transport to "policy reduced
  microplastic pollution." Mandatory crosslink.
- **Advocacy/interested sources.** Both plastics-industry-adjacent
  reassurance and campaign-group alarm exist; neither may be a scored leaf's
  sole support, and authoritative weight-of-evidence bodies (SAPEA, WHO,
  Koelmans risk-framework group) are preferred.

**Alternatives considered and deferred** (carried from the UPF proposal, not
rejected — candidates for later sessions): *school vouchers / education
savings accounts* (high salience but a large values component); *return-to-office
mandates and productivity* (evidence currently lopsided); *minimum-wage
employment effects* and *nuclear energy safety/cost*. Microplastics was
selected this session because the user named it and because it is an unusually
clean example of the presence-vs-harm conflation the tool is built to
separate.

## Why now

Human-tissue detection studies (placenta 2021, blood 2022, arterial plaque
2024) have made "microplastics in the body" a headline while the harm
evidence remains early and contested; measurement methods are still being
standardised (VAMAS/ISO); and regulators are acting (EU intentionally-added
microplastic restriction, 2023) on a hazard whose population-level human
effect authoritative reviews say is not yet demonstrated. The gap between
public alarm and the evidence base is wide and current — the condition this
tool is designed for.
