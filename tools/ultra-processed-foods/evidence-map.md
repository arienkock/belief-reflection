# Ultra-Processed Foods and Health — evidence map (v1)
_Belief-investigation tool. Generated by scripts/compose-tool.py. Nodes are **questions**; only leaf nodes carry a claim you rate yourself. The tool shows evidence and its limits — it does **not** compute a verdict or update your number for you._

> **What this map leaves out (scope):** Authored dimensions: the validity of the category, the observational association, whether processing or nutrients drive it, controlled-trial causal evidence, and restriction/labelling policy. DELIBERATELY NOT COVERED (invisible to this tool): 'food addiction' as a clinical construct; individual additives and their toxicology (emulsifiers, nitrites, non-sugar sweeteners) as separate claims; environmental/food-system sustainability; children and adolescents as a separate population (this map is adult-focused, where the RCT and cohort evidence sits); eating-disorder outcomes; industry political economy and marketing-to-children beyond what a policy finding carries; and whether restricting ultra-processed food is JUSTIFIED all things considered — a values question this tool records no verdict on. Absence is a scope choice, not evidence of no effect. Two definitional boundaries: 'processed' is not 'ultra-processed' (NOVA group 3 vs group 4), and nutrient-targeted policy (e.g. sugar labels) is not processing-targeted policy — findings must not silently swap them.

### ▸ What does the evidence show about ultra-processed foods and health — and is it the processing, or the nutrients the processing carries?
<sub>`n_root` · topic · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
> **Depends on:** 'Bad for you' is not one measurable quantity. The literature separately measures whether the ultra-processed (NOVA) category is even reliably definable, the size of the observational disease association, whether that association is about processing or about the nutrients and lifestyle it travels with, whether controlled trials show processing itself causes harm, and what ultra-processed-food policy measurably changes — with sharply different degrees of consensus. Public and political debate routinely lets confidence on one of these stand in for all of them; this tree exists to keep them apart.
- **◑ COMPLICATES** · review · strong — There is no single measurement of 'are ultra-processed foods bad for you'. The literature separately measures whether the NOVA category can even be applied consistently, the size of the observational disease association, whether that association reflects processing or the nutrients it carries, whether controlled-feeding trials show processing itself causes overeating, and what policy changes. The strongest observational synthesis (an umbrella review of 45 pooled analyses covering ~9.9 million people) documents broad associations while explicitly stating that randomised trials are still needed and that residual confounding is the most pertinent limitation.
  <sub>src: Lane et al. 2024, Ultra-processed food exposure and adverse health outcomes: umbrella review of epidemiological meta-analyses, BMJ 384:e077310. [lane-umbrella] · caveat: Umbrella review of observational meta-analyses; the authors note it 'did not consider specific confounder or mediator adjustments'.</sub>
- **◑ COMPLICATES** · review · moderate — Evidence quality is sharply uneven across the sub-questions. The observational association rests on millions of participants; the causal question rests on a handful of small, short feeding trials (a 2024 systematic review found only four qualifying RCTs, with no significant effect in 30 of 42 outcomes); and the classification underpinning all of it fails inter-rater reliability tests. Confidence earned on the best-measured sub-question does not transfer to the others.
  <sub>src: Dominguez-Rodriguez et al. 2024, Ultra-processed foods consumption and health-related outcomes: a systematic review of randomized controlled trials, Frontiers in Nutrition 11:1421728. [rct-review] · caveat: Systematic review of only four heterogeneous RCTs with 20-40% dropout in three of them.</sub>
  ### ▸ Is 'ultra-processed' a valid, usable category — or a label that can't be applied consistently and adds little beyond known nutrients?
  <sub>`n_concept` · dimension · 🟡 thin</sub>
  > ⚠ classification validity is itself contested — read the association leaves in that light
  > **Depends on:** Two separable questions: whether trained raters even assign the same foods to the NOVA 'ultra-processed' group (reliability), and whether the category predicts harm beyond what conventional nutrient profiling already captures (added value). A category can be unreliable yet still track something real, or reliable yet redundant with nutrients — the two failures are different and are scored separately.
  > *Scope note:* Covers the classification's reliability and its added value over nutrient profiling. Does not adjudicate the NOVA system's four-group structure as a whole, nor rival processing-classification schemes (SIGA, IARC/IFIC) except where a finding carries them.
  - **◑ COMPLICATES** · theoretical · moderate — This dimension has two distinct failure modes that must not be conflated. A classification can be UNRELIABLE (different raters disagree on what is ultra-processed) or REDUNDANT (reliable but adding nothing over conventional nutrient profiling). They are separate questions with separate evidence, and a category could fail either test while passing the other.
    <sub>src: Braesco et al. 2022, Ultra-processed foods: how functional is the NOVA system?, European Journal of Clinical Nutrition 76:1245-1253. [nova-reliability] · caveat: A framing of the two failure modes, not a measurement.</sub>
    ### • Can the NOVA 'ultra-processed' category be applied consistently by different classifiers?
    <sub>`n_concept_reliable` · leaf · 🟡 thin</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **The NOVA 'ultra-processed' category can be applied consistently — different trained classifiers reliably assign the same foods to the same group.**
    > ⚠ rests on a single dedicated reliability study (Braesco 2022) | Evidence base is US/European/Brazilian-heavy; external validity of inter-rater reliability to other cultural and food-system settings is uncertain. | no qualifying FOR located — one-sided
    > **Depends on:** Reliability is measured, not asserted: inter-rater agreement statistics (e.g. Fleiss' kappa) on classifying the same food lists. Notably, agreement in the main reliability study stayed low even when full ingredient information was available — so it is not simply a fixable data-access problem. Low reliability undercuts what any pooled UPF–outcome association can mean, because the exposure is being measured inconsistently.
    - **▼ AGAINST** · observational · moderate — When 159 and 177 food-and-nutrition specialists independently assigned foods to NOVA groups, inter-rater agreement was poor: Fleiss' kappa was 0.32 for 120 marketed products (with ingredient lists) and 0.34 for 111 generic items (without). Agreement stayed low even when full ingredient information was available, so it is not a fixable data-access problem. The authors conclude 'current NOVA criteria do not allow for robust and functional food assignments.'
      <sub>effect: Fleiss' kappa 0.32 (marketed) / 0.34 (generic) — poor agreement · src: Braesco et al. 2022, Ultra-processed foods: how functional is the NOVA system?, European Journal of Clinical Nutrition 76:1245-1253.     ### • Does ultra-processed status predict harm beyond what conventional nutrient profiling already captures?
    <sub>`n_concept_beyond` · leaf · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Ultra-processed status carries information about health risk over and above conventional nutrient profiling (sugar, salt, saturated fat, energy density) — the processing dimension adds predictive value.**
    > ⚠ exposure and diet-quality adjustment both derive from self-reported diet — over- or under-adjustment cannot be excluded
    > **Depends on:** Depends on whether UPF–outcome associations persist once nutrient composition or diet-quality indices are adjusted for. Persistence supports added value; full attenuation implies the label is a proxy for nutrients already measurable. This is the added-value framing; the closely related n_assoc_confound leaf asks the same statistical question from the association's side — they are scored separately because 'the concept is redundant' and 'this particular association is confounded' are different claims.
    - **▲ FOR** · review · moderate — A review of 37 prospective-cohort studies (91 models, 87 outcomes) found that 64 of 66 significant ultra-processed-food–outcome associations remained significant after adjustment for diet quality or dietary pattern, and 136 of 142 individual adjustments did not explain the association (all-cause mortality 15/15 unchanged; CVD 29/31; cancer 8/8). The authors conclude 'the adverse consequences of UPFs are independent of dietary quality or pattern.'
      <sub>effect: 64/66 associations survive diet-quality adjustment · src: Dicken & Batterham 2022, The Role of Diet Quality in Mediating the Association between Ultra-Processed Food Intake, Obesity and Health-Related Outcomes: A Review of Prospective Cohort Studies, Nutrients 14(1):23. [dietquality-review] · caveat: Reviews observational studies whose exposure and confounders are both from self-reported diet; 'unchanged after adjustment' can also reflect over- or under-adjustment, and residual confounding remains. · ⚡ F-bey-2, F-bey-3, F-con-2</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · review · moderate — A 2024 systematic review of UPF randomized trials concludes that 'the added value of classifying foods based on their industrial processing compared to traditional nutrient-based systems remains an unresolved controversy' — the experimental evidence does not establish that the processing dimension adds information beyond nutrients. Combined with the poor reliability of the classification, the case that the category itself carries independent predictive value is weak on trial evidence.
      <sub>effect: qualitative; added value 'unresolved' · src: Dominguez-Rodriguez et al. 2024, Frontiers in Nutrition 11:1421728. [rct-review] · caveat: Only four RCTs; absence of demonstrated added value is not proof of no added value. · ⚡ F-bey-1</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · observational · moderate — The large 2024 US cohort found its ultra-processed-food–mortality association became 'less pronounced after overall dietary quality was taken into account, suggesting that dietary quality has a stronger influence on long term health than ultra-processed food consumption' — evidence that, in at least this dataset, much of the signal tracks nutrients the category is supposed to add value beyond. The added-value question and the confounding question are the same statistic read from two sides.
      <sub>src: Fang et al. 2024, Association of ultra-processed food consumption with all cause and cause specific mortality, BMJ 385:e078476. [bmj-cohort] · caveat: 'Less pronounced' is not 'eliminated'; adjusting for diet quality may over-control if nutrients lie on the causal pathway of processing. · ⚡ F-bey-1</sub>
  ### ▸ How is ultra-processed food consumption related to disease — how large, and is it the processing?
  <sub>`n_assoc` · dimension · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
  > ⚠ near-entirely observational, self-reported diet (FFQ) throughout — residual confounding and healthy-user bias are live
  > **Depends on:** Depends on WHICH question (does an association exist and how large, vs. does it survive adjustment for nutrients and confounders, vs. is it uniform across UPF types), and on measurement (almost all evidence is self-reported diet via food-frequency questionnaires, with residual confounding and healthy-user bias live throughout).
  > *Scope note:* Captures the magnitude of the observational association, whether it reflects processing after adjustment, and whether it is concentrated in UPF subgroups. Cause-in-the-controlled-trial sense lives under n_cause; the classification's validity lives under n_concept.
  - **◑ COMPLICATES** · theoretical · moderate — Three distinct questions govern this dimension: whether an association exists and how large, whether it survives adjustment for nutrients and confounders, and whether it is uniform or concentrated in subgroups. Almost all of the evidence is observational and rests on self-reported diet (food-frequency questionnaires), so residual confounding and healthy-user bias are live throughout — the same measurement error affects both the exposure and the attempts to adjust it away.
    <sub>src: Lane et al. 2024, BMJ 384:e077310 (residual-confounding discussion). [lane-umbrella] · caveat: A methodological framing of the dimension, not a measurement.</sub>
    ### • Is higher ultra-processed food consumption associated with worse health outcomes at a meaningful magnitude?
    <sub>`n_assoc_magnitude` · leaf · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Higher ultra-processed food consumption is associated with worse health outcomes (all-cause mortality, cardiovascular disease, type-2 diabetes, obesity) at a magnitude that is practically meaningful, not trivially small.**
    > ⚠ observational — causal interpretation is not licensed | graded evidence tiers reflect statistical strength/consistency, not causation
    > **Depends on:** The existence of a broad association is not seriously disputed; the live questions are magnitude and interpretation. Umbrella-level syntheses report associations across dozens of outcomes, but individual well-controlled cohorts report modest hazard ratios (e.g. ~+4% all-cause mortality, top vs bottom quartile). Findings must report the number, not the adjective.
    - **▲ FOR** · review · strong — An umbrella review of 45 pooled analyses (~9.9 million people) graded several associations as 'convincing' or 'highly suggestive': cardiovascular-disease mortality (RR 1.50, 95% CI 1.37-1.63), type-2 diabetes (dose-response RR 1.12, 1.11-1.13), common mental disorders (OR 1.53, 1.43-1.63), all-cause mortality (RR 1.21, 1.15-1.27), and obesity (OR 1.55, 1.36-1.77). The breadth and consistency of higher-tier associations is not trivially small.
      <sub>effect: CVD-mortality RR 1.50; all-cause mortality RR 1.21; obesity OR 1.55 · src: Lane et al. 2024, Ultra-processed food exposure and adverse health outcomes: umbrella review of epidemiological meta-analyses, BMJ 384:e077310. [lane-umbrella] · caveat: Observational inputs; graded evidence-tiers reflect statistical strength/consistency, not causation; residual confounding flagged by the authors as most pertinent. · ⚡ F-cau-1, F-geo-2, F-rel-1, F-sub-1</sub>
    - **▲ FOR** · review · strong — An independent scoping review for the Nordic Nutrition Recommendations 2023 (12 systematic reviews including 5 meta-analyses, plus 44 studies) concluded the evidence linking ultra-processed food to weight gain, cardiovascular disease, type-2 diabetes, and all-cause mortality is 'strong enough to support dietary recommendations to limit' its consumption — a second synthesis team reaching a concordant magnitude verdict.
      <sub>effect: qualitative; concordant across four major outcomes · src: Juul & Bere 2024, Ultra-processed foods - a scoping review for Nordic Nutrition Recommendations 2023, Food & Nutrition Research 68. [nordic-scoping] · caveat: Synthesizes overlapping observational cohorts with the umbrella review (partial non-independence on the underlying primary studies); still correlational at base.</sub>
    - **▲ FOR** · review · strong — An updated dose-response meta-analysis of 18 prospective cohort studies (>1.1 million participants, 173,107 deaths) found a 15% higher all-cause mortality risk for the highest versus lowest UPF intake, with a linear dose-response of ~10% higher risk per 10 percentage-point increment of UPF as share of total diet. This extends the umbrella-review signal with newer cohort data in a single-exposure meta-analytic framework.
      <sub>effect: +15% all-cause mortality highest vs lowest UPF quartile; +10% per 10% UPF increment (dose-response) · src: Nguyen et al. 2025, Ultra-processed food consumption and all-cause mortality: a systematic review and dose-response meta-analysis of prospective cohort studies, Systematic Reviews (Springer) PMC11874696. [upf-mortality-meta-2025] · caveat: 18 cohorts are predominantly US, European, and Brazilian; the included Korean cohort (KoGES-HEXA) showed a null result — heterogeneity is present. Self-reported diet throughout; dose-response does not establish causation. · ⚡ F-geo-2</sub>
    - **▲ FOR** · quasi-experimental · moderate — An attributable-mortality modelling study across eight countries (Australia, Brazil, Canada, Chile, Colombia, Mexico, UK, USA) estimated that 4–14% of premature deaths (age <70) are attributable to ultra-processed food consumption, using a dose-response risk function derived from a meta-analysis of prospective cohorts. The four Latin American countries in the sample (Brazil, Chile, Colombia, Mexico) showed estimates in the 4–10% range, extending the mortality signal beyond the usual high-income Western pool.
      <sub>effect: 4–14% of premature deaths attributable to UPF across 8 countries; dose-response RR ~1.03 per 10% UPF increment · src: Passos et al. 2025, Premature Mortality Attributable to Ultra-Processed Food Consumption in 8 Countries, American Journal of Preventive Medicine. [attributable-mortality-global] · caveat: Modelling study: attributable fractions depend on the assumed dose-response RR (drawn from meta-analyses dominated by US/European cohorts) and on dietary-survey inputs; assumes generalisability of the risk function to each country's food environment and population, which is not established. Not a direct cohort observation.</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · observational · moderate — In one of the most rigorously controlled cohorts (74,563 women + 39,501 men, 48,193 deaths over 30+ years), the highest-versus-lowest quartile of total ultra-processed intake carried only a 4% higher all-cause mortality (1536 vs 1472 deaths per 100,000 person-years), with no association for cardiovascular, cancer, or respiratory mortality. The pooled higher-tier relative risks and this modest, well-adjusted cohort estimate are both 'the magnitude' — which one to weigh is a judgement about design.
      <sub>effect: +4% all-cause mortality (top vs bottom quartile); null for CVD/cancer/respiratory · src: Fang et al. 2024, Association of ultra-processed food consumption with all cause and cause specific mortality, BMJ 385:e078476. [bmj-cohort] · caveat: Highly educated health-professional cohort (limits generalisability but also confounding); self-reported diet; a small relative risk over a large population can still be many deaths.</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · observational · moderate — A large South Korean prospective cohort (KoGES-HEXA; 113,576 adults; median follow-up 10.6 years; 3,456 deaths) found no statistically significant association between ultra-processed food intake and all-cause, cancer, or cardiovascular-disease mortality. This is the largest single East Asian cohort study on the question and constitutes genuine evidence against the universality of the magnitude finding — not a null caused by low power.
      <sub>effect: null — no statistically significant association for all-cause, cancer, or CVD mortality · src: Kim et al. 2023, Ultra-processed food consumption and mortality in the Korean Genome and Epidemiology Study Health Examinee (KoGES-HEXA) cohort, PLOS ONE 18(5):e0284285. Subtype-stratified analysis not available to test this. · ⚡ F-geo-1, F-mag-1</sub>
    ### • Does the association reflect processing itself, rather than being explained by nutrients and confounding?
    <sub>`n_assoc_confound` · leaf · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **The observed ultra-processed-food–disease association reflects a processing effect that survives adjustment for nutrient quality and confounders — it is not fully explained by the sugar/salt/fat/energy-density of these foods or by healthy-user bias.**
    > ⚠ the pivotal processing-vs-nutrients dispute — carried live in both directions | adjustment can over-control if nutrients lie on the causal pathway of processing | Evidence base is US/Nordic/UK/French-heavy; external validity to other settings is uncertain.
    > **Depends on:** The pivotal and most genuinely two-sided empirical leaf. Reviews disagree: some report associations 'remain significant and unchanged in magnitude' after diet-quality adjustment; others (including a large 2024 cohort) find the association substantially attenuates once overall dietary quality is controlled. Because the exposure and confounders are both derived from self-reported diet, residual confounding cannot be excluded. Adjustment can also over-control if nutrients are on the causal pathway.
    - **▲ FOR** · review · moderate — The review of 37 prospective cohorts found 64 of 66 significant ultra-processed-food associations survived adjustment for diet quality, and 136 of 142 adjustments did not explain the association — including all-cause mortality (15/15 unchanged), CVD (29/31), cancer (8/8), and type-2 diabetes (7/7). On this evidence the association is not merely a proxy for poor nutrient quality; something about processing appears to contribute independently.
      <sub>effect: 136/142 diet-quality adjustments left the association intact · src: Dicken & Batterham 2022, Nutrients 14(1):23. [dietquality-review] · caveat: Observational throughout; 'survives adjustment' cannot distinguish a true processing effect from unmeasured confounding correlated with UPF intake. · ⚡ F-con-2</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · observational · moderate — The large 2024 US cohort reached the opposite conclusion for mortality: the ultra-processed-food association 'was less pronounced after overall dietary quality was taken into account, suggesting that dietary quality has a stronger influence on long term health than ultra-processed food consumption.' Where this well-powered study adjusted carefully, nutrient quality — not processing per se — carried most of the signal.
      <sub>effect: association attenuates after diet-quality adjustment · src: Fang et al. 2024, BMJ 385:e078476. [bmj-cohort] · caveat: A single cohort; adjusting for diet quality could over-control if the nutrient profile is part of how processing acts. 'Less pronounced' is not 'zero'. · ⚡ F-bey-1, F-con-1</sub>
    - **◑ COMPLICATES** · theoretical · moderate — Both directions inherit an unfixable limitation: exposure, confounders, and diet-quality indices are all derived from the same self-reported dietary instruments, so neither 'survives adjustment' nor 'attenuates' can settle causation. The umbrella review's authors name residual confounding as 'perhaps most pertinent', and only controlled-feeding trials can break the deadlock — which is why this leaf's answer partly waits on the causal dimension.
      <sub>src: Lane et al. 2024, BMJ 384:e077310 (residual-confounding limitation). [lane-umbrella] · caveat: A framing of the shared limitation, not a measurement.</sub>
    ### • Are harms concentrated in specific ultra-processed subgroups rather than uniform across all ultra-processed food?
    <sub>`n_assoc_subgroup` · leaf · 🟡 thin</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **The health harms of ultra-processed food are concentrated in specific subgroups (notably processed meats and sugar-sweetened beverages) rather than spread uniformly across all ultra-processed foods.**
    > ⚠ subgroup breakdown comes from a single cohort | 'no significant association' for a subgroup is not evidence of safety | no independent AGAINST located — one-sided
    > **Depends on:** If true, a total-UPF exposure average mixes harmful, neutral, and possibly protective subgroups, so the aggregate understates the worst categories and overstates the benign ones — which changes what a single 'ultra-processed' policy target would capture. Depends on the subgroup breakdown within cohorts; independent corroboration across cohorts is the test of whether the pattern is robust.
    - **▲ FOR** · review · strong — An IHME Burden of Proof analysis (Nature Medicine, 2025), pooling GBD-anchored prospective data, found processed-meat consumption associated with a ≥11% T2D risk increase and ≥7% colorectal cancer risk increase per serving increment, and sugar-sweetened beverages associated with ≥8% T2D and ≥2% ischaemic heart disease risk increases — each receiving a two-star certainty rating (weak-to-moderate evidence). This is the first large-scale GBD-anchored synthesis to quantify subgroup-specific risk magnitudes within the UPF category.
      <sub>effect: processed meat: ≥11% T2D risk increase, ≥7% colorectal cancer risk per serving; SSBs: ≥8% T2D, ≥2% IHD risk increase (two-star certainty) · src: IHME Burden of Proof Study (GBD Collaborators), Burden of proof risk function for processed meat/SSBs and NCDs, Nature Medicine (2025). Two-star certainty reflects weak-to-moderate evidence, not certainty; estimates are lower bounds at the 5th percentile of the risk distribution.</sub>
    - **▲ FOR** · observational · moderate — In the 2024 US cohort, the total-ultra-processed association was driven by specific subgroups rather than spread evenly: meat/poultry/seafood-based ready-to-eat products showed the strongest and most consistent association with mortality, followed by sugar- and artificially-sweetened beverages, dairy-based desserts, and ultra-processed breakfast foods, while several other subgroups (fats/condiments/sauces, packaged sweet and savory snacks, ready-to-eat dishes) showed no significant association. The category is not internally uniform.
      <sub>effect: processed meat strongest; several UPF subgroups null · src: Fang et al. 2024, BMJ 385:e078476 (subgroup analyses). [bmj-cohort] · caveat: 'No significant association' for a subgroup is not evidence it is safe; subgroup analyses have less power and this concentration pattern comes from a single cohort. · ⚡ F-mag-1</sub>
    - **◑ COMPLICATES** · review · moderate — The umbrella review's broad, higher-tier associations are stated for TOTAL ultra-processed exposure across many outcomes and cannot, on their own, distinguish a genuinely concentrated harm (a few bad subgroups) from a diffuse one. A total-UPF exposure measure that mixes harmful, neutral, and possibly protective subgroups will understate the worst categories and overstate the benign ones — so a single 'ultra-processed' policy target may be aiming at a heterogeneous mixture.
      <sub>src: Lane et al. 2024, BMJ 384:e077310 (total-UPF exposure). [lane-umbrella] · caveat: Pooled total-exposure associations neither confirm nor refute concentration; an independent cohort with a full subgroup breakdown was not located under the protocol.</sub>
  ### ▸ Do controlled trials show ultra-processing itself causes harm, independent of nutrients?
  <sub>`n_cause` · dimension · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
  > ⚠ only four qualifying RCTs, small and short — generalisability limited
  > **Depends on:** Controlled-feeding trials can hold nutrient composition roughly constant and vary processing, isolating a processing effect that observational designs cannot. But such trials are few, small, and short, use highly controlled or metabolic-ward settings, and their mechanism (energy density, hyper-palatability, eating rate) is debated — a real intake effect does not by itself pin the causal lever on 'processing' as opposed to texture and energy density that nutrient policy could also target.
  > *Scope note:* Only the energy-intake / weight causal claim is scored — it is where controlled trials exist. Longer-term disease endpoints (CVD, cancer) cannot be tested in feeding trials and are covered observationally under n_assoc. Additive-specific mechanisms are out of scope (see root coverage note).
  - **◑ COMPLICATES** · theoretical · moderate — Controlled-feeding trials can hold nutrient composition roughly constant and vary only processing, isolating a processing effect that observational designs cannot — but they are few, small, and short, run in metabolic-ward or motivated-volunteer settings, and their mechanism is contested. A real intake effect does not by itself locate the causal lever on 'processing' as opposed to energy density, texture, or eating rate, which nutrient- or design-based policy could also target.
    <sub>src: Hall et al. 2019, Cell Metabolism 30(1):67-77 (mechanism discussion). [hall-rct] · caveat: A framing of what feeding trials can and cannot isolate, not a standalone measurement.</sub>
    ### • Do ultra-processed diets cause excess energy intake or weight gain independent of nutrient composition?
    <sub>`n_cause_intake` · leaf · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Ultra-processed diets cause excess energy intake and weight gain relative to less-processed diets, independent of nutrient composition (calories presented, macronutrients, sugar, sodium, fibre matched).**
    > ⚠ metabolic-ward / motivated-volunteer settings | the causal lever (processing vs energy density / eating rate) is contested | Evidence base is US-heavy (single NIH inpatient RCT); no qualifying non-Western RCT found. External validity to other settings is uncertain. | fast-moving: Re-check the Nature Medicine critique/reply thread on Dicken 2025 before republish — the dispute was active at authoring time.
    > **Depends on:** Depends on the trial: the landmark inpatient RCT matched presented calories, energy density, macronutrients, sugar, sodium and fibre and still found ~+500 kcal/day; a 2025 outpatient crossover found more weight loss on minimally-processed diets following healthy-eating guidance. Both are small; the second drew formal published critiques. Whether the effect generalises beyond metabolic-ward or motivated-volunteer settings, and whether the operative lever is 'processing' or energy density and eating rate, are open.
    - **▲ FOR** · experimental · moderate — In a randomized crossover inpatient trial (n = 20, two weeks per arm), diets matched for presented calories, energy density, macronutrients, sugar, sodium, and fibre still produced about 500 kcal/day more ad libitum intake on the ultra-processed diet, with a 0.9 kg weight gain versus an equivalent loss on the unprocessed diet. Because participants could not choose different foods and the nutrients were matched, this is the strongest evidence that processing itself drives overeating.
      <sub>effect: +~500 kcal/day; +0.9 kg on UP vs equivalent loss on unprocessed · src: Hall et al. 2019, Ultra-Processed Diets Cause Excess Calorie Intake and Weight Gain: An Inpatient Randomized Controlled Trial of Ad Libitum Food Intake, Cell Metabolism 30(1):67-77. [hall-rct] · caveat: n = 20, two weeks, metabolic ward — generalisability limited; the authors note slight protein differences could explain up to half the intake gap, and faster eating rate is implicated, so 'processing' may act via energy density/texture rather than processing as such. · ⚡ F-cau-3, F-mag-1</sub>
    - **▲ FOR** · experimental · moderate — The longest UPF feeding trial to date (randomized crossover, 55 adults, 8-week arms, both diets following UK healthy-eating guidance) found roughly twice the weight loss on the minimally-processed diet (-2.06%) versus the ultra-processed diet (-1.05%) — an independent second trial pointing the same way, and notably even when both diets were nutritionally 'healthy'.
      <sub>effect: -2.06% (MPF) vs -1.05% (UPF) body weight at 8 weeks · src: Dicken et al. 2025, Ultra-processed or minimally processed diets following healthy dietary guidelines on weight and cardiometabolic health: a randomized, crossover trial, Nature Medicine.     ### • Do front-of-pack warning labels reduce purchases of the targeted products?
    <sub>`n_policy_labels` · leaf · 🟡 thin</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Front-of-pack warning labels reduce purchases of the targeted products.**
    > ⚠ strongest evidence targets NUTRIENTS (sugar/salt/fat), not processing — transportability to a UPF-specific label is an assumption | no independent AGAINST located — one-sided
    > **Depends on:** Depends on the outcome (purchases, measured here, vs. consumption or health, not) and — critically — on the target: the strongest evidence (Chile's 2016 law) concerns NUTRIENT warning labels (high-in sugar/salt/saturated-fat/calories), not NOVA processing labels, and bundles labelling with marketing and school-sales restrictions. It is strong evidence that nutrient-targeted front-of-pack labels change purchasing; its transportability to a processing-specific label is an assumption, not a finding.
    - **▲ FOR** · quasi-experimental · moderate — Chile's 2016 Law of Food Labeling — mandatory front-of-pack 'high in' warning labels — was followed by a 23.7% fall in purchases of high-in sugar-sweetened beverages versus the pre-regulation-trend counterfactual, tracked across 2,383 households and ~70,000 household-months. This is strong policy-natural-experiment evidence that front-of-pack warning labels change what people buy.
      <sub>effect: -23.7% high-in sugar-sweetened-beverage purchases vs counterfactual · src: Taillie et al. 2020, An evaluation of Chile's Law of Food Labeling and Advertising on sugar-sweetened beverage purchases from 2015 to 2017, PLOS Medicine 17(2):e1003015. [chile-labels] · caveat: The labels flagged NUTRIENTS (added sugar/sodium/saturated-fat thresholds), NOT the ultra-processed category, and came bundled with marketing and school-sales restrictions; outcome is purchases, not consumption or health. · ⚡ F-rel-1</sub>
    - **◑ COMPLICATES** · theoretical · thin — The label evidence's transportability to ultra-processed-specific policy is an assumption, not a finding. Chile targeted nutrients that are straightforward to measure and threshold; the ultra-processed category is defined by processing and, per the reliability leaf, cannot be applied consistently — so a warning label reading 'ultra-processed' would face a classification problem that a 'high in sugar' label does not. Evidence that nutrient labels work is not evidence that a processing label would.
      <sub>src: Taillie et al. 2020, PLOS Medicine 17(2):e1003015 (nutrient-threshold basis); Braesco et al. 2022 (classification reliability).
