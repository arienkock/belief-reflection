# Social Media and Adolescent Mental Health — evidence map (v1)
_Belief-investigation tool. Generated by scripts/compose-tool.py. Nodes are **questions**; only leaf nodes carry a claim you rate yourself. The tool shows evidence and its limits — it does **not** compute a verdict or update your number for you._

> **What this map leaves out (scope):** Authored dimensions: individual association & causation, the population trend, mechanisms, restriction policies. DELIBERATELY NOT COVERED (invisible to this tool): content-specific harms with their own literatures (cyberbullying prevalence, grooming/exploitation, CSAM, pro-self-harm content exposure); 'problematic use'/addiction as a clinical construct; effects on adults; polarization and civic effects; physical-health outcomes; platform-by-platform design comparisons; privacy/data-rights harms; and whether age bans are JUSTIFIED all things considered — a values question this tool records no verdict on. Absence is a scope choice, not evidence of no effect. Note also: 'screen time' is not 'social media use'; findings based on general screen measures are labelled as such and must not be read as social-media-specific.

### ▸ What does the evidence say about social media's effects on adolescent mental health — and on which question, exactly?
<sub>`n_root` · topic · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
> **Depends on:** 'Harmful' is not one measurable quantity. The literature separately measures the individual-level association, whether it is causal, whether it explains the population trend since ~2010, the mechanism, and what restriction policies change — with sharply different degrees of consensus. Public debate routinely lets confidence on one of these stand in for all of them; this tree exists to keep them apart.
- **◑ COMPLICATES** · review · strong — The literature never measures 'is social media harming teenagers?' directly. It separately measures the individual-level association, whether that association is causal, whether social media explains the population trend since ~2010, the mechanism, and what restriction policies change — each with its own data, methods, and degree of consensus. The 2024 National Academies consensus report is explicit that these questions have different answers: it affirms that specific design features can harm some young people while declining to endorse a population-level causal conclusion.
  <sub>src: National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine 2024, Social Media and Adolescent Health (consensus report). [NAS-2024] · caveat: Consensus synthesis across studies of varying design and quality.</sub>
- **◑ COMPLICATES** · review · moderate — Evidence quality is sharply uneven across the sub-questions. Correlational associations are measured in hundreds of studies; causal evidence is a handful of quasi-experiments and RCTs, mostly on adults; population-trend attribution rests largely on timing correlations; and national-ban evidence barely exists. Most research to date is correlational and has generated a mix of small positive, negative, and null associations — confidence earned on the best-measured sub-question does not transfer to the others.
  <sub>src: Odgers & Jensen 2020, Annual Research Review, Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry 61:336-348. [odgers-review] · caveat: Narrative annual review, not a formal systematic search; pre-2020 literature.</sub>
  ### ▸ How is social media use related to individual adolescents' mental health — and is the relationship causal?
  <sub>`n_assoc` · dimension · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
  > ⚠ mostly correlational — causal interpretation is not licensed | self-reported use dominates — device-log associations run weaker
  > **Depends on:** Depends on WHICH question (correlation magnitude vs. causal effect), HOW use is measured (self-report inflates associations relative to device logs), WHO (average adolescent vs. vulnerable subgroups and developmental windows), and WHICH direction of effect (harms and benefits can coexist in the same population).
  > *Scope note:* Captures individual-level association, causal evidence, heterogeneity, and benefits. Does not cover clinical 'problematic use' constructs, and platform-specific differences (e.g. image-based vs. text-based) are visible only where a finding happens to carry them.
  - **◑ COMPLICATES** · theoretical · moderate — Two distinctions govern this whole dimension. First, association is not causation: distressed adolescents may use more social media rather than the reverse, and the leading review concludes the correlational designs 'do not offer a way of distinguishing cause from effect'. Second, measurement matters: most studies rely on self-reported use, and associations measured from device logs run substantially weaker than self-report-based ones.
    <sub>src: Odgers & Jensen 2020, J. Child Psychology & Psychiatry 61:336-348; Orben & Przybylski 2019, Nature Human Behaviour. [odgers-review] · caveat: A methodological framing, not a direct measurement.</sub>
    ### • Is heavier social media use meaningfully associated with worse adolescent depression and anxiety?
    <sub>`n_assoc_magnitude` · leaf · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Heavier social media use is associated with worse depression/anxiety in adolescents at a magnitude that is practically meaningful, not trivially small.**
    > ⚠ mostly correlational — causal interpretation is not licensed | self-reported use dominates — device-log associations run weaker | Evidence base is Global-North-heavy (>70% of studies; 0% Africa) and the pooled association is null in the Global South (Ghai et al. 2023). External validity to other settings is uncertain.
    > **Depends on:** The live dispute is magnitude, not existence: pooled correlations around r 0.2 (problematic-use measures higher) vs. specification-curve work bounding variance explained near zero. What counts as 'practically meaningful' is itself contested; findings must report the number, not the adjective.
    - **▲ FOR** · review · strong — Meta-analysis of problematic social media use in adolescents and young adults finds moderate, statistically significant pooled correlations with depression (r = 0.27), anxiety (r = 0.35), and stress (r = 0.31). On the 'problematic use' construct — use with addiction-like features, not mere time spent — the association is not trivially small.
      <sub>effect: pooled r = 0.27 (depression), 0.35 (anxiety) · src: Shannon et al. 2022, Problematic Social Media Use in Adolescents and Young Adults: Systematic Review and Meta-analysis, JMIR Mental Health 9(4):e33450. [jmir-problematic-meta] · caveat: 'Problematic use' is a clinical-adjacent construct; results do not transfer to time-spent measures. Cross-sectional, self-report inputs — no causal direction. · ⚡ F-geo-1, F-mag-4</sub>
    - **▲ FOR** · review · strong — A meta-analysis of 2020-2024 evidence on social-networking-site use and mental disorders in adolescents and young adults finds a significant positive pooled association of small-to-moderate magnitude (r ≈ 0.22) between exposure to SNS-related risk factors and mental-health problems — the recent literature, taken together, does not support a zero effect.
      <sub>effect: pooled r ≈ 0.22 · src: Associations Between Social Media Use and Mental Disorders in Adolescents and Young Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Recent Evidence, Behavioral Sciences 15(11):1450 (2025). [recent-sns-meta] · caveat: Heterogeneous designs and exposure definitions pooled; overwhelmingly correlational, self-report inputs.</sub>
    - **▲ FOR** · review · strong — A dose-response meta-analysis of time spent on social media and adolescent depression finds depression risk rising with hours of daily use — the association is not flat across the usage range, which is what a purely spurious link would more easily produce.
      <sub>effect: monotone dose-response of depression risk in daily hours · src: Time Spent on Social Media and Risk of Depression in Adolescents: A Dose-Response Meta-Analysis (2022; PMC9103874). [dose-response-meta] · caveat: Observational inputs with self-reported time; dose-response is consistent with but does not establish causation (reverse causation can also be dose-shaped).</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · observational · moderate — Specification-curve analysis across three large adolescent datasets shows digital-technology use explains at most ~0.4% of the variation in well-being, and that defensible analytic choices can produce positive, negative, or null associations from the same data. Companion time-use-diary analyses find comparably tiny associations. On population-representative time-spent measures, the association is real but trivially small.
      <sub>effect: ≤0.4% of well-being variance explained · src: Orben & Przybylski 2019, The association between adolescent well-being and digital technology use, Nature Human Behaviour 3:173-182; Orben & Przybylski 2019, Screens, Teens, and Psychological Well-Being, Psychological Science 30(5). [orben-przybylski] · caveat: Measures broad digital-technology/screen use more than social media specifically; cross-sectional variance-explained is not an estimate of any causal effect. · ⚡ F-cau-1, F-het-1, F-mag-1</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · review · moderate — The leading annual research review concludes that the most rigorous large-scale preregistered studies report small associations between daily digital-technology use and adolescent well-being that 'are unlikely to be of clinical or practical significance', and that the wider literature is a mix of conflicting small positive, negative, and null associations.
      <sub>effect: small; 'unlikely to be of clinical or practical significance' · src: Odgers & Jensen 2020, Annual Research Review, J. Child Psychology & Psychiatry 61:336-348. [odgers-review] · caveat: Narrative review; covers literature to 2019, before the strongest quasi-experimental work and the newest platforms.</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · review · moderate — A scoping review and meta-analysis of the adolescent depression / social-media literature (34 articles, 2018-2020) found an overall pooled association of r=.14 (95% CI .09-.20) but showed it is geographically contingent: the link 'was positive and significant in the Global North but null and nonsignificant in the Global South'. The evidence base is heavily non-representative — over 70% of studies sampled Global North populations (26% North America, 26% Europe, 15% Oceania, 21% Asia incl. Japan, 9% Middle East, 3% Latin America, 0% Africa). It challenges the claim that a practically-meaningful association is a general adolescent phenomenon rather than a Global-North one.
      <sub>effect: pooled r=.14 (95% CI .09-.20) overall; positive/significant in the Global North, null in the Global South; >70% of studies Global North (0% Africa) · src: Ghai S, Fassi L, Awadh F, Orben A. Lack of sample diversity in research on adolescent depression and social media use: a scoping review and meta-analysis. Clinical Psychological Science 2023;11(5):759-772. doi:10.1177/21677026221114859. [ghai-diversity] · caveat: The Global South null rests on relatively few Global South studies (3% Latin America, 0% Africa), so it is an absence-of-evidence as much as evidence-of-absence there. Author overlap: this cluster shares an author (Orben) with the orben-przybylski cluster already carried AGAINST on this leaf, so the two AGAINST clusters are NOT fully independent — flagged in the audit, not treated as clean corroboration. · ⚡ F-mag-1</sub>
    ### • Does social media use causally worsen adolescent mental health?
    <sub>`n_assoc_causal` · leaf · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Social media use causally worsens adolescent mental health.**
    > ⚠ strongest designs use 2000s college students and adult samples — transportability to today's adolescents not established | Causal designs use Western (US/UK) samples; no qualifying LMIC causal design located. Evidence base is high-income-heavy; external validity to other settings is uncertain.
    > **Depends on:** Depends on the causal contrast: platform introduction (quasi-experiments), short-run individual deactivation (RCTs, mostly adult samples), and lifelong non-adoption answer different questions. A causal effect in one contrast does not establish the others.
    - **▲ FOR** · quasi-experimental · moderate — Exploiting the staggered rollout of Facebook across US colleges (2004-2006) as a natural experiment, Facebook's arrival worsened student mental health and increased self-reported academic impairment due to poor mental health; mechanism evidence points to unfavorable social comparison. This is the strongest causal identification in the literature, and it finds harm.
      <sub>effect: negative effect on a standardized mental-health index; qualitative here · src: Braghieri, Levy & Makarin 2022, Social Media and Mental Health, American Economic Review 112(11):3660-3693. [econ-quasiexp] · caveat: College students in 2004-2006, one platform, US only — transportability to 2020s adolescents and today's algorithmic platforms not established. · ⚡ F-att-1, F-ben-1, F-mag-4</sub>
    - **▲ FOR** · experimental · moderate — In a large randomized experiment (n ≈ 2,743), paying users to deactivate Facebook for the four weeks before the 2018 US midterms increased subjective well-being and offline activity. If removal improves well-being, continued use plausibly imposes a real ongoing cost — experimental evidence consistent with causal harm.
      <sub>effect: positive SWB gain from 4-week deactivation · src: Allcott, Braghieri, Eichmeyer & Gentzkow 2020, The Welfare Effects of Social Media, American Economic Review 110(3). [allcott-gentzkow] · caveat: Adult sample — transportability to adolescents is an assumption; short-run withdrawal is not the effect of never adopting.</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · review · strong — The National Academies consensus committee, after reviewing the full literature, concluded that the available evidence 'did not support the conclusion that social media causes changes in adolescent health at the population level' — while acknowledging that specific design features can harm some young people. The strongest institutional synthesis declines to certify the causal claim.
      <sub>src: National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine 2024, Social Media and Adolescent Health. [NAS-2024] · caveat: Absence of certified population-level causation is not evidence of absence; the committee's bar is deliberately high.</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · review · thin — A preregistered meta-analysis of 27 social-media reduction and exposure experiments found a pooled effect around d ≈ 0.09 whose confidence interval includes zero — no reliable average causal effect of experimentally changing social media use on mental health.
      <sub>effect: pooled d ≈ 0.09, 95% CI includes zero · src: Ferguson 2024, Do social media experiments prove a link with mental health: a methodological and meta-analytic review, Psychology of Popular Media. [reduction-rct-pool] · caveat: DISPUTED: published critiques (Rausch & Haidt 2025, SSRN 5224958) allege errors in effect sizes, sample sizes, and inclusion criteria; a 2025 reanalysis of the same pool finds benefits emerge for longer interventions. Carry this finding together with its dispute.</sub>
    - **◑ COMPLICATES** · theoretical · moderate — The causal question is really three: what platform INTRODUCTION did (quasi-experiments on rollout), what short-run individual DEACTIVATION does (RCTs, mostly adults), and what never-adopting or population-wide absence would do (essentially unmeasured). Positive evidence in one contrast does not establish the others — deactivation experiments, for instance, leave the participant in a world where everyone else is still online.
      <sub>src: National Academies 2024 (evidence-limitations discussion); Allcott et al. 2020 AER (interpretation discussion). [NAS-2024] · caveat: A framing of the evidence's limits, not a measurement.</sub>
    ### • Are harms concentrated in vulnerable subgroups and developmental windows rather than uniform?
    <sub>`n_assoc_hetero` · leaf · 🟡 thin</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Negative effects of social media are concentrated in specific subgroups and developmental windows (notably girls in early adolescence) rather than spread uniformly.**
    > ⚠ mostly correlational — causal interpretation is not licensed | Subgroup evidence (girls, early adolescence) is Anglosphere/Nordic; no LMIC subgroup replication located. Evidence base is high-income-heavy; external validity to other settings is uncertain. | no independent AGAINST located — one-sided
    > **Depends on:** If true, population-average effects understate risk for the sensitive groups and overstate it for everyone else — which changes what both the null-average findings and the alarm-raising findings mean.
    - **▲ FOR** · observational · moderate — Analyses of two UK datasets (n = 84,011; longitudinal n = 17,409) identify distinct developmental windows in which higher social media use predicts lower life satisfaction a year later: ages 11-13 for girls, 14-15 for boys, and 19 for both sexes. Sensitivity is not uniform across adolescence — and the girls' window sits in early adolescence.
      <sub>effect: negative use→satisfaction prediction confined to specific age/sex windows · src: Orben, Przybylski, Blakemore & Kievit 2022, Windows of developmental sensitivity to social media, Nature Communications 13:1649. [orben-przybylski] · caveat: Observational; estimated (not logged) use; same research group whose specification-curve work anchors the tiny-average-association case — the two are consistent (small average, concentrated windows), not contradictory. · ⚡ F-mag-4</sub>
    - **▲ FOR** · observational · moderate — The post-2010 rises in US adolescent depressive symptoms, suicide-related outcomes, and suicide rates are concentrated among girls, and new-media screen time is more strongly associated with mental-health problems among girls — the trend data's sex asymmetry matches the heterogeneity hypothesis.
      <sub>effect: rises and associations concentrated among girls · src: Twenge, Joiner, Rogers & Martin 2018, Clinical Psychological Science 6(1):3-17. [twenge] · caveat: Correlational trend data; author is the most prominent proponent of the harm hypothesis — treat as one input, not a neutral referee.</sub>
    - **▲ FOR** · quasi-experimental · moderate — Causal evidence from Norwegian school smartphone bans shows benefits concentrated exactly where the heterogeneity hypothesis predicts: reduced psychological-symptom healthcare use among girls, and GPA/exam gains for girls, strongest for low-socioeconomic-status students. Removing the exposure helped the predicted subgroups most.
      <sub>effect: girls' math exam ≈ +0.22 SD; psych-care visits down among girls · src: Abrahamsson 2024, Smartphone Bans, Student Outcomes and Mental Health, NHH Dept. of Economics Discussion Paper 01/2024 (SSRN 4735240). [nordic-ban] · caveat: Indirect: policy-response heterogeneity, and smartphones-in-school, not social media per se; working paper at scan date.</sub>
    ### • Does social media provide real mental-health-relevant benefits to some adolescents?
    <sub>`n_assoc_benefits` · leaf · 🟡 thin</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Social media provides real mental-health-relevant benefits to some adolescents — community, connection, and support, especially for marginalized youth.**
    > ⚠ single-source FOR: NAS-2024 is the only qualifying FOR cluster | largely qualitative and self-report evidence | Benefit evidence centres on Western marginalized-youth samples; no LMIC benefit study located. Evidence base is high-income-heavy; external validity to other settings is uncertain. | no independent AGAINST located — one-sided
    > **Depends on:** Depends on WHICH adolescents (marginalized youth, e.g. LGBTQ+ teens in unsupportive environments, are the strongest case) and whether benefits are weighed within the same population that carries the harms — both can be true at once.
    - **▲ FOR** · review · strong — The National Academies consensus report treats benefits as findings, not talking points: social media creates opportunities for community and support among marginalized youth — including LGBTQ+ adolescents in unsupportive environments — and provides everyday connection and enjoyment for the large majority of adolescent users.
      <sub>effect: qualitative; strongest for marginalized youth · src: National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine 2024, Social Media and Adolescent Health.     - **◑ COMPLICATES** · review · moderate — The same review literature that undercuts large average harms also undercuts large average benefits: associations between use and well-being are a mix of small positive, small negative, and null. Benefits and harms plainly coexist within the same population — the honest reading is heterogeneity in both directions, not a net verdict.
      <sub>src: Odgers & Jensen 2020, J. Child Psychology & Psychiatry 61:336-348. [odgers-review] · caveat: Narrative review; pre-2020 literature.</sub>
  ### ▸ Did adolescent mental health really deteriorate after ~2010, and is social media the driver?
  <sub>`n_trend` · dimension · 🟡 thin</sub>
  > ⚠ Anglosphere/Nordic-centric evidence — external validity not established
  > **Depends on:** Two separable claims: that the deterioration is real (vs. changed reporting, screening, and diagnostic practice) and that social media adoption is its principal cause. The second presupposes the first but does not follow from it — the timing coincidence plus an individual-level association is not, by itself, population-level attribution.
  > *Scope note:* Covers the reality and the attribution of the post-2010 trend in high-income countries. Competing explanations (economic insecurity, academic pressure, parenting shifts, opioid-era spillovers) are visible only as they appear inside attribution findings; they are not separately scored.
  - **◑ COMPLICATES** · theoretical · moderate — Two claims travel together in public debate but are evidentially separate: that adolescent mental health really worsened after ~2010, and that social media caused it. The first can be true while the second is unproven — a timing coincidence plus an individual-level association is not population-level attribution. Rival explanations (economic insecurity, academic pressure, changed diagnostic practice) are rarely tested head-to-head.
    <sub>src: Odgers & Jensen 2020, JCPP; National Academies 2024. [odgers-review] · caveat: A framing, not a measurement.</sub>
    ### • Has adolescent mental health genuinely deteriorated since ~2010?
    <sub>`n_trend_real` · leaf · 🟡 thin</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Adolescent mental health has genuinely deteriorated since ~2010; the rise is not an artifact of changed reporting, screening, or diagnostic practice.**
    > ⚠ no full AGAINST located — artifact hypothesis carried via COMPLICATES only | Anglosphere/Nordic-centric evidence — external validity not established | GBD 2021 (F-geo-3) shows the rise is high-income-concentrated (fastest in North America) with global rates broadly stable; the deterioration is not established as global. External validity to other settings is uncertain. | no independent AGAINST located — one-sided
    > **Depends on:** Depends on the outcome series: self-report symptom scales are vulnerable to reporting change; hospitalizations and suicide statistics are harder to explain away but have their own recording issues. Measurement-invariance tests are the direct evidence on the artifact question.
    - **▲ FOR** · observational · moderate — US adolescents' depressive symptoms, suicide-related outcomes, and suicide RATES all rose after 2010, especially among girls. Because the rise includes objectively recorded outcomes (suicide deaths), it cannot be fully explained by greater help-seeking, awareness, or willingness to report distress.
      <sub>effect: concurrent rises across self-report and objective series post-2010 · src: Twenge, Joiner, Rogers & Martin 2018, Clinical Psychological Science 6(1):3-17; Twenge 2020, Psychiatric Research and Clinical Practice 2:19-25. [twenge] · caveat: US-centric; suicide statistics have their own recording issues; author is the leading proponent of the social-media explanation.</sub>
    - **▲ FOR** · observational · moderate — A Norwegian study directly tested the measurement-artifact objection: the depressive-symptoms instrument was measurement-invariant across 2010-2019 (the scale meant the same thing over time), the few non-invariant parameters had negligible impact, and latent mean symptom scores genuinely rose among girls from 2014. Where the artifact hypothesis has been formally tested, the increase survived.
      <sub>effect: latent-mean rise among girls from 2014; invariance held · src: Psychological Medicine 2024. Time trends in adolescent depressive symptoms from 2010 to 2019 in Norway: real increase or artifacts of measurements? (PMC11578914). [nordic-trend] · caveat: One country, one instrument; the artifact hypothesis could still hold elsewhere or for other measures. · ⚡ F-geo-3</sub>
    - **◑ COMPLICATES** · modelling · moderate — The same GBD 2021 analysis (204 countries, 1990-2021) reframes the deterioration question geographically: the rise in adolescent depression is concentrated in high-income regions and fastest in High-income North America (EAPC 1.04 prevalence), while global age-standardised rates were broadly stable and several middle-/low-income regions declined. This is consistent with the leaf being scoped to high-income countries, but it establishes that a 'genuine deterioration since ~2010' is a high-income-regional phenomenon in the modelled burden data, not a worldwide one — neither confirming nor refuting the reporting-artifact hypothesis, but bounding the trend's external validity.
      <sub>effect: rise concentrated in high-income regions (High-income North America fastest, EAPC 1.04); global rates broadly stable 1990-2021 · src: Scientific Reports 2025 (s41598-024-84843-w; Europe PMC PMC11830012). The Burden of adolescent depression and the impact of COVID-19 across 204 countries and regions from 1990 to 2021: results from the 2021 Global Burden of Disease study. [ihme-gbd] · caveat: Modelled GBD prevalence partly incorporates the same survey instruments whose validity the artifact hypothesis questions, so it cannot independently adjudicate 'genuine vs reporting change'; it speaks to geographic distribution, not to the artifact question. GBD-derived (cluster ihme-gbd). · ⚡ F-real-2</sub>
    - **◑ COMPLICATES** · theoretical · thin — The artifact concern is nonetheless live: most trend studies use raw sum scores without testing whether their measures are invariant over time — a prerequisite for meaningful trend inference that the Norwegian authors note is routinely skipped. Reporting norms, screening practices, and diagnostic thresholds all changed over the same decade; how much of the international rise they explain is not settled.
      <sub>src: Methodological framing in: Psychological Medicine 2024 Norway invariance study (PMC11578914). [nordic-trend] · caveat: No standalone quantitative artifact-hypothesis source located under the protocol at authoring time — this caution is carried from within a pro-real-increase source; the AGAINST side of this leaf is genuinely thin.</sub>
    ### • Is social media adoption the principal driver of the post-2010 deterioration?
    <sub>`n_trend_attrib` · leaf · 🟡 thin</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Social media adoption is the principal driver of the post-2010 deterioration in adolescent mental health.**
    > ⚠ correlational timing evidence — causal interpretation is not licensed | GBD 2021 (F-geo-2) shows social media diffused globally while the deterioration did not (global rates stable; several LMIC regions declining). Evidence base is high-income-heavy; external validity to other settings is uncertain. | FOR single-cluster: twenge supplies all 2 FOR findings
    > **Depends on:** This is the map's most conflation-prone claim: it inherits unearned confidence from the association and causation leaves. Scaling an individual-level effect into the principal population driver requires additional assumptions (dose, coverage, absence of comparable rival shocks) that must be argued, not assumed.
    - **▲ FOR** · observational · moderate — The timing and shape of the deterioration match the exposure: adolescent depressive symptoms, self-harm, and suicide rose precisely as smartphone-plus-social-media adoption saturated (post-2010/2012), the rises are concentrated among girls (the heaviest social-media users), and new-media screen time is associated with the same outcomes at the individual level. No rival explanation matches the timing, cross-national breadth, and sex pattern as well, on this argument.
      <sub>effect: timing + sex-pattern concordance; correlational · src: Twenge, Joiner, Rogers & Martin 2018, Clinical Psychological Science; Twenge 2020, Psychiatric Research and Clinical Practice (mechanisms review). [twenge] · caveat: Correlational timing evidence from the hypothesis's leading proponent; the FOR side of this leaf is single-cluster — flagged in the audit. · ⚡ F-att-3, F-cau-1, F-geo-2</sub>
    - **▲ FOR** · review · moderate — The proposed mechanisms are articulated and individually plausible: displacement of sleep and in-person interaction, upward social comparison, and cyberbullying exposure — each with its own supporting literature — jointly connecting population-level adoption to population-level distress.
      <sub>effect: qualitative (mechanism synthesis) · src: Twenge 2020, Increases in Depression, Self-Harm, and Suicide Among U.S. Adolescents After 2012 and Links to Technology Use: Possible Mechanisms, Psychiatric Research and Clinical Practice 2:19-25. [twenge] · caveat: Mechanism plausibility is not attribution; same cluster as F-att-1 (no independent corroboration added).</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · review · strong — The National Academies consensus review examined this literature and did not support the conclusion that social media causes changes in adolescent health at the population level — the direct institutional verdict on the attribution claim, from the strongest available synthesis tier.
      <sub>src: National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine 2024, Social Media and Adolescent Health. [NAS-2024] · caveat: High evidentiary bar; 'not supported' is not 'refuted'. · ⚡ F-att-1</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · modelling · strong — A Global Burden of Disease 2021 analysis of adolescent depression across 204 countries and regions (1990-2021) found global age-standardised prevalence, incidence and DALY rates remained broadly stable over the period, with rising trends concentrated in high-income regions — High-income North America grew fastest (EAPC 1.04 prevalence, 1.30 incidence, 1.19 DALYs) — while several middle- and low-income regions (East Asia most markedly, plus South Asia and the Caribbean) showed DECREASING rates. Because smartphone and social-media adoption diffused globally over the same window, a globally-diffused exposure paired with a non-global (indeed high-income-concentrated) outcome undercuts the claim that social media is the principal population-level driver.
      <sub>effect: global rates stable 1990-2021; High-income North America fastest (EAPC 1.04 prevalence); East/South Asia and Caribbean decreasing · src: Scientific Reports 2025 (s41598-024-84843-w; Europe PMC PMC11830012). The Burden of adolescent depression and the impact of COVID-19 across 204 countries and regions from 1990 to 2021: results from the 2021 Global Burden of Disease study. [ihme-gbd] · caveat: GBD estimates are modelled and, in low-surveillance settings, rest on sparse primary data with wide uncertainty; regional rate DIVERGENCE is more robust than any single region's absolute level. GBD-derived (cluster ihme-gbd); do not treat as independent of other GBD-based analyses. · ⚡ F-att-1</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · review · moderate — Scaling objection: the best-measured individual-level associations are small and bidirectionally confounded, which makes social media an underpowered candidate for PRINCIPAL driver of a large population-level deterioration without additional strong assumptions. The review literature emphasizes that correlational designs cannot distinguish cause from effect at any scale.
      <sub>src: Odgers & Jensen 2020, J. Child Psychology & Psychiatry 61:336-348. [odgers-review] · caveat: An inference from association magnitudes, not a direct test of the attribution claim.</sub>
  ### ▸ Through what pathway would social media harm adolescents — and which pathways are actually evidenced?
  <sub>`n_mech` · dimension · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
  > ⚠ screens ≠ social media — much of this literature measures devices generally
  > **Depends on:** Proposed pathways differ in testability. Sleep displacement is separately measurable and is scored here. Social comparison and algorithmic amplification are evidenced mainly as mechanism findings inside other studies (e.g. the Facebook-rollout comparison evidence) and via expert synthesis — they surface in this map as findings and crosslinks on the causal leaves, not as scored leaves of their own.
  > *Scope note:* Only sleep displacement is scored. Social comparison, algorithmic amplification, and displacement of in-person time are DELIBERATELY NOT scored as leaves — the literature evidences them as embedded mechanism findings rather than as independently testable claims with good-faith evidence on both sides; forcing them into scored leaves would manufacture false balance. They remain visible via findings and crosslinks on n_assoc_causal. This is a Phase 1 scope decision, revisitable if the mechanism literature matures.

  ### ▸ What do restriction policies — school phone bans, individual deactivation, age-based platform bans — measurably change?
  <sub>`n_policy` · dimension · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
  > ⚠ fast-moving policy literature — several key sources are 2024–2026 working papers
  > **Depends on:** Depends on the intervention's dose and scope (school-hours phone removal vs. weeks of personal deactivation vs. society-wide age limits), on the outcome measured (use, wellbeing, grades), and on enforcement.
