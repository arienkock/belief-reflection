# Immigration — evidence map (v1)
_Belief-investigation tool. Generated by scripts/compose-tool.py. Nodes are **questions**; only leaf nodes carry a claim you rate yourself. The tool shows evidence and its limits — it does **not** compute a verdict or update your number for you._

> **What this map leaves out (scope):** Authored dimensions: labour market, public finances, innovation, social outcomes. DELIBERATELY NOT COVERED (invisible to this tool): housing & rents, congestion of public services, gains to employers/capital owners (the counterpart to any wage effect), effects on origin countries (brain drain / remittances), and political/electoral effects. Absence is a scope choice, not evidence of no effect.

    ### • Does immigration increase crime?
    <sub>`n_social_crime` · leaf · 🟢 well-evidenced</sub>
    *You rate your confidence in:* **Immigration increases crime.**
    > ⚠ mostly correlational — causal interpretation is not licensed | Evidence base is US/European-heavy; Global-South immigration-crime evidence is largely qualitative or forced-displacement-focused. External validity to other settings is uncertain.
    > **Depends on:** None
    - **▲ FOR** · observational · moderate — Across 38 European countries (Eurostat 2017–2020), immigration is significantly and POSITIVELY associated with some specific offences once socioeconomic factors are included — notably sexual-violence rates (r ≈ 0.20) and theft. The association is country-level, cross-sectional and correlational, and is offence-specific rather than a uniform crime increase.
      <sub>effect: r≈0.20 sexual violence; positive for theft (country-level, cross-sectional) · src: Crime, Law and Social Change 2024 — 38 European countries, Eurostat 2017–2020. [EuroCrime-2024] · caveat: Correlational; selection and confounding (where immigrants settle, who is selected to migrate) are not fully ruled out. · ⚡ F-cr-2, F-cr-5</sub>
    - **▼ AGAINST** · observational · strong — In the US the immigration–crime association is weak and not positive.
